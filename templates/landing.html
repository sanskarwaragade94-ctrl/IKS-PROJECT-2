1. Acharya Pingala

Acharya Pingla Rishi

Acharya Pingala (c. 3rd-2nd century BCE) was an ancient Indian mathematician and grammarian. He is the author of the Chandah-shastra, the earliest known Sanskrit treatise on prosody, the study of poetic meters.

The binary connection appears in Pingala's work on combinations of light (Laghu) and heavy (Guru) syllables. To map every possible rhythmic pattern, he developed a system that is functionally equivalent to the modern binary numeral system.

He was the first to describe the mathematical properties of 2^n combinations. His work also laid the groundwork for what we now compare with Pascal's Triangle, known in India as Meru Prastara, to calculate combinations of meters.

While modern binary uses 0 and 1, Pingala used L (Laghu) and G (Guru). His work proves that the concept of mapping a sequence of two states to represent numerical values was understood in India millennia ago.

2. Nasta Algorithm

Row number to binary sequence

The Nasta (or Nashta) algorithm, found in Pingala's ancient Indian text Chandah-shastra (c. 3rd century BC), is a method to determine the specific binary sequence corresponding to a particular row number in a combinations table.

Purpose: It reverses the process of finding the position of a pattern and identifies the pattern itself from the row number.

Process: Start with the desired row number and divide the number by 2.

If divisible: place 1 representing laghu.

If not divisible: place 0 representing guru, add 1 to the number, and then divide by 2.

Iteration: Repeat the division process with the quotient until the binary sequence of the required length is obtained.

Significance: This demonstrates an early understanding of binary numbers and combinatorial mathematics to calculate combinations of long and short syllables.

3. Uddishta Algorithm

Binary sequence to row number

Uddishta is an ancient Indian algorithmic method, formulated by Acharya Pingala (c. 3rd-2nd century BCE) in his Chandah-shastra, to determine the specific position or row number of a given binary sequence within a prastara. It is the reverse process of Nasta, which finds the sequence from a row number.

Context: Used in Vedic mathematics and Sanskrit prosody using binary principles where Laghu is 0 and Guru is 1.

Start: Begin with 1.

Scan: Scan the sequence from right to left.

Operation: For each digit, if the digit is 1, multiply the current value by 2; if it is 0, multiply by 2 and subtract 1 in the traditional interpretation.

Example: For 1011, the algorithm builds the final position step by step from the rightmost digit to the left.

Significance: It demonstrates advanced understanding of binary mathematics and combinations by ancient Indian mathematicians long before modern computing.
